Index

A couple of worker craft have just finished securing the interplanetary torchship under the starship shield and are hurrying to dock with it. Inside, the numerous crew members are dressing in their provisional emergency suits and fastening themselves to their designated seats. Excitement and focus are clear on their faces, with everyone glued to their monitors. Outside, media drones from all around the local system point their cameras at the departing starship. This is a once-in-a-decade departure, with visiting starships staying for only a few years before their own decade-spanning voyages.

With the crew secured and a countdown finished, the fusion drives start slowly ramping up. A couple of hours of tiny flow follow, just to test and engage the plumbing, where the ship feels a tiny amount of acceleration. Then, a brilliant flash occurs, far outshining the comparatively distant local star and everything else. Engines capable of accelerating millions of tons of a fully loaded ship necessarily shine brighter than some stars, necessarily wielding the power to destroy anything unlucky enough to be glanced by the plumes. This is expected and joyous, and the system's media make sure to capture this brilliant torch in the outer system. The crew are joyous too, as this means their systems are working correctly. The newfound 1 g of acceleration presses the crew into the backs of their seats and starts to visibly move the departure station in the field of view. The trip has started.

This will take decades. The crew stayed in their seats for five hours as a contingency measure, but once the arbitrary time limit passed, the start was considered successful. They originally reached their seats by floating through the air, but now they stand up with their feet pointing toward the engines. The habitation rings don't need to rotate for now; all gravity is provided by acceleration. A couple dozen or so crew are freed from their occupations managing the spacecraft during those crucial moments. Every departure in their hundreds of years of lifetime rings bittersweet, with temporary acquaintances in the latest system leaving only a mark in their memories. Some of the crew decided to stay behind to claim the new system as their home. There are some new faces; all will have plenty of time to get acquainted in the following decades onboard.

Individual scenes transition into an accelerating montage. It takes 150 days for them to reach their maximum speed of 0.4 c. After this period, the engines shut off and the ship as a whole enters weightlessness once again. To compensate, the habitation rings start spinning up: neighboring ones, among the four, spin in opposite directions to cancel the net torque. Relative time passage dilates, but only ever so slightly - no more than 1%. This shortens the perceived time of travel by only a small margin. It will take 24 years for the ship to arrive at its destination, and they've got plenty of personal time to spare.

In most cases, the specialist maintainers need only to occasionally check the automatic reports while computer systems take care of the harsh environment of relativistic travel. Every hydrogen particle turns into extreme alpha radiation, and every grain of dust threatens a nuclear-grade explosion. This is why the most important part of the ship for now is the shield supply infrastructure, continuously replenishing the ablative material and diffusing the heat of collisions. Point-defense lasers occasionally fire from under the shield to evaporate particles detected by radar. Radiator panels at the back shine with cast-iron red, working hard to remove the heat produced by the shield and the reactors.

An accelerating time montage centers on an ordinary paper calendar placed on a desk in someone's quarters. At first, it is slow enough to showcase the various routines of different members, but the days soon start flying faster and faster, moving the mark of the day by weeks, and then months. Behind the calendar, in the background, we see various celebrations, local fads, interior renovations, and lighting changes, all over the course of many years. Until they arrive, this is their own universe.
